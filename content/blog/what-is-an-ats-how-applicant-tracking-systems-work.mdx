# What Is an ATS? How Applicant Tracking Systems Work (And How to Beat Them)

You've sent out dozens of applications and heard nothing back. Your resume looks great — clean design, solid experience, the right job titles. So what's going wrong? The answer, more often than not, is that your resume never made it to a human. An applicant tracking system (ATS) filtered it out before anyone ever saw it. Understanding how ATS software works is no longer optional — it's one of the most important things a job seeker can know in today's market.

## What Is an Applicant Tracking System?

An applicant tracking system (ATS) is software that employers use to collect, sort, and manage job applications at scale. Think of it as a digital gatekeeper that sits between your resume and the hiring manager's inbox.

When a company posts a job opening and receives hundreds — sometimes thousands — of applications, a human being simply cannot review every single one first. ATS software automates that initial screening. It parses your resume, extracts key information, scores your application against the job requirements, and ranks candidates accordingly. Only the top-scoring resumes get passed along to a real person.

### Who Uses ATS Software?

Nearly every mid-to-large employer uses some form of ATS. According to widely cited industry research, over 98% of Fortune 500 companies use applicant tracking systems. But it's not just big corporations — small and mid-sized businesses increasingly rely on platforms like Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Taleo, and iCIMS to manage hiring pipelines.

If you're applying through an online job portal rather than handing your resume directly to someone, there's a very good chance an ATS is involved.

### What Does ATS Software Actually Do?

Here's the basic flow:

1. **Resume parsing** — The ATS reads your resume and breaks it into structured data: contact info, work history, education, skills, certifications.
2. **Keyword matching** — It compares your resume content against the job description, flagging relevant terms and phrases.
3. **Scoring and ranking** — Candidates are scored based on how well their resume matches the job requirements.
4. **Filtering** — Resumes below a certain threshold may never be seen by a recruiter at all.

The problem? ATS systems are not perfect readers. They struggle with complex formatting, graphics, tables, headers and footers, and non-standard fonts. A beautifully designed resume with columns and icons can get completely scrambled during parsing — and a scrambled resume scores poorly, even if your qualifications are excellent.

## Why Your Resume Might Be Getting Rejected by ATS

This is where most job seekers lose the game without realizing it. Your resume might be losing points not because of your experience, but because of how it's formatted or what words you're using (or not using).

### The Keyword Problem

ATS systems are heavily keyword-dependent. If the job description says "project management" and your resume says "oversaw projects," the system may not connect the two. You need to mirror the language used in the job posting — within reason and honestly.

Here's how to approach keywords strategically:

- Read the job description carefully and identify the most repeated skills and qualifications
- Include those exact phrases in your resume where they genuinely apply to your experience
- Use both spelled-out versions and acronyms where relevant (e.g., "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)")
- Don't keyword stuff — modern ATS systems and the humans reviewing results are both watching for that

### The Formatting Problem

Complex resume designs that look impressive to the human eye are often unreadable to ATS parsers. Common culprits include:

- **Tables and columns** — These can cause parsing errors where text gets jumbled or skipped entirely
- **Text boxes** — Content inside text boxes is often invisible to ATS software
- **Headers and footers** — Critical contact information placed here may not be parsed correctly
- **Graphics and icons** — Skill bars, profile photos, and decorative elements add zero value to an ATS and can interfere with parsing
- **Unusual fonts** — Stick to clean, standard fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Georgia

The safest structure is a single-column, clean layout with clearly labeled sections: Summary, Work Experience, Education, Skills. Simple and readable wins every time.

## How to Optimize Your Resume for ATS

Now that you understand how the system works, here's how to work with it — not against it.

### Tailor Every Application

Generic resumes are ATS kryptonite. Every job posting is slightly different, and the ATS is trained on that specific posting. That means you need to customize your resume for each role — at minimum, adjusting your skills section and tweaking your bullet points to reflect the language in the job description.

Yes, this takes more time. But one tailored resume that gets past the ATS is worth more than fifty generic ones that don't.

### Use Standard Section Headings

Don't get creative with your section labels. "Professional Journey" might feel more personal, but the ATS is looking for "Work Experience." Stick with conventional headings:

- **Summary** or **Professional Summary**
- **Work Experience** or **Experience**
- **Education**
- **Skills** or **Core Competencies**
- **Certifications** (if applicable)

### Submit in the Right File Format

When in doubt, submit your resume as a **.docx** file. While many ATS systems can read PDFs, some older systems parse Word documents more reliably. Always check the job posting for specific instructions — and follow them exactly.

### Quantify Your Achievements

ATS systems are getting smarter, and so are the recruiters reviewing results. Where possible, use numbers to describe your impact. "Increased student engagement by 35%" is stronger than "improved student outcomes" — both for ATS relevance and human interest.

## What ATS Can't Do (And Why Humans Still Matter)

It's worth keeping this in perspective: the ATS is a filter, not a decision-maker. Its job is to narrow a pool of 500 applicants down to a manageable number for human review. Once your resume makes it through, you're back to impressing a real person.

That means your resume still needs to be compelling, clear, and human-readable — not just ATS-compliant. Think of ATS optimization as getting in the door. Your actual experience and how you present it closes the deal.

Also worth noting: ATS software has real limitations. It misses strong candidates. It rewards keyword matching over genuine qualification. Many companies are aware of this and supplement ATS screening with recruiter review. Your goal is simply to give yourself the best possible chance of making the cut.
