# Cover Letter Example: How to Write One That Actually Gets You Hired

A strong cover letter can be the difference between landing an interview and getting lost in a pile of applications. Yet most job seekers either skip it entirely or fire off something so generic it might as well be invisible. If you've been searching for a solid cover letter example to guide your own writing, you're in the right place. This article walks you through exactly what a compelling cover letter looks like, breaks down each component, and gives you a real-world example you can adapt for your next application.

## Why Cover Letter Examples Matter More Than You Think

Many candidates underestimate the power of a well-crafted cover letter. Hiring managers often spend less than 30 seconds scanning a resume — but a cover letter that opens with a hook can stop them in their tracks. Studying a quality cover letter example helps you understand tone, structure, and the kind of language that resonates with recruiters.

A cover letter isn't just a summary of your resume. It's your chance to show personality, demonstrate genuine interest in the role, and connect the dots between your experience and the company's specific needs. Think of it as your professional handshake before the interview.

## Anatomy of an Effective Cover Letter

Before diving into a full example, it helps to understand the key building blocks that make a cover letter work.

### The Opening Line

Forget "I am writing to express my interest in..." — it's the career equivalent of elevator music. Instead, open with something specific. Reference the company by name, mention a recent milestone they achieved, or lead with your strongest qualification.

### The Value Proposition Paragraph

This is the heart of your cover letter. In two to three sentences, explain what you bring to the table and why it matters to this employer specifically. Quantify your impact wherever possible. Numbers cut through vague claims and make your accomplishments credible.

### The Connection Paragraph

Show that you've done your homework. Mention something about the company's mission, culture, or recent work that genuinely excites you. This signals that you're not mass-applying — you actually want *this* job.

### The Closing Call to Action

End confidently. Don't beg or undersell yourself. Express enthusiasm for the conversation and state that you look forward to discussing how you can contribute.

## Cover Letter Example You Can Use as a Template

Below is a complete, ready-to-adapt cover letter example for a marketing coordinator role. Notice how each section does specific work.

---

**Jane Mitchell**
jane.mitchell@email.com | LinkedIn: linkedin.com/in/janemitchell | (555) 204-8812

**March 15, 2025**

**Hiring Manager**
Bright Horizon Media
New York, NY

---

Dear Hiring Manager,

When I saw Bright Horizon Media's recent campaign for the Elevate Foundation go viral across three continents, I knew I wanted to be part of the team behind work like that. As a marketing coordinator with four years of experience driving digital campaigns that increased lead generation by 62%, I'm confident I can bring both creative energy and measurable results to your team.

In my current role at Momentum Agency, I manage end-to-end content calendars for eight clients simultaneously, coordinate cross-functional teams of designers and copywriters, and track campaign KPIs using HubSpot and Google Analytics. Last quarter, I spearheaded a rebranding initiative that grew our client's Instagram following from 12,000 to 47,000 in 90 days — without a paid ads budget. I thrive in fast-paced environments where strategy and creativity have to work hand in hand.

What draws me specifically to Bright Horizon Media is your commitment to purpose-driven storytelling. Your recent partnership with nonprofit organizations aligns directly with work I've been building toward — campaigns that don't just sell but genuinely connect. I believe marketing at its best changes how people see the world, and that's the kind of team I want to grow with.

I'd love the opportunity to discuss how my background in content strategy and campaign management can support your upcoming Q3 initiatives. Thank you for your time and consideration — I look forward to connecting.

Warm regards,
Jane Mitchell

---

## What Makes This Cover Letter Example Work

Let's break down why this example is effective, so you can replicate the strategy — not just copy the words.

### It Opens with a Specific Hook

Jane doesn't start with her name or job title. She opens by referencing something real about the company. This immediately signals that she's done research and isn't sending a copy-paste letter to 50 employers.

### It Leads with Impact, Not Duties

Notice that Jane mentions a 62% increase in lead generation before she ever mentions her job title. Recruiters are drawn to results. Leading with what you've accomplished — rather than what your job description says — immediately sets you apart.

### It Speaks to the Employer's Needs

Every sentence in a strong cover letter answers an unspoken question: *What's in it for us?* Jane doesn't just say she's passionate about marketing — she connects that passion to the company's specific work and values.

### It's the Right Length

This cover letter hits around 280 words — long enough to make a case, short enough to respect the reader's time. Aim for three to four focused paragraphs. A cover letter that runs over a page is rarely read in full.

## Common Cover Letter Mistakes to Avoid

Even with a great example in front of you, it's easy to fall into familiar traps.

- **Rehashing your resume verbatim.** Your cover letter should add context, not repeat bullet points.
- **Using vague language.** Phrases like "team player" or "hard worker" say nothing. Replace them with specific examples.
- **Forgetting to customize.** A generic cover letter is worse than no cover letter at all. Always tailor it to the company and role.
- **Neglecting the closing.** A weak sign-off undercuts everything that came before it. Be direct and confident.
- **Skipping proofreading.** One typo in a cover letter sends a message you don't want to send. Read it aloud, then run it through a grammar checker.

## Adapting This Cover Letter Example for Different Industries

The structure of a strong cover letter holds across industries, but the tone and emphasis will shift. A cover letter for a creative role can afford to be more conversational and personality-driven. One for a finance or legal position should lean more formal and data-focused. For tech roles, highlight specific tools, languages, or methodologies early.

The key is always the same: know your audience, lead with value, and make it clear why you — specifically — are the right fit for this role.

A great cover letter doesn't require you to be a gifted writer. It requires you to know what the employer needs and communicate clearly why you can deliver it. Use this cover letter example as your starting point, adapt it to your own experience and voice, and approach each application as a genuine opportunity to make a first impression worth remembering. The investment of 20 extra minutes on a tailored cover letter can open doors that a resume alone never will.
