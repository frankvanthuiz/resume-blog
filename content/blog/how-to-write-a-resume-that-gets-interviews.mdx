Landing an interview starts long before you walk into the room. It starts with a resume that stops a recruiter mid-scroll and makes them think: *I need to talk to this person.*

Most resumes don't do that. They're generic, cluttered, or optimized for no one. This guide will show you exactly how to write a resume that gets interviews — step by step.

## TL;DR

- Structure your resume with contact info, professional summary, work experience, skills, and education in a single clean column to pass ATS systems.
- Write a professional summary using this formula: [role] + [years of experience] + [key achievement] + [what you're looking for].
- Quantify every achievement with numbers, percentages, or measurable results instead of listing generic duties.
- Optimize for applicant tracking systems by using simple formatting, relevant keywords from job descriptions, and avoiding tables, graphics, and images.

## Start With the Right Structure

A resume that gets results follows a clear, proven structure. Recruiters spend an average of 7 seconds scanning a resume before deciding whether to read further. Your layout needs to make those seconds count.

Use this order:

1. **Contact information** — name, phone, email, LinkedIn, city
2. **Professional summary** — 2–3 sentences that sell your value
3. **Work experience** — most recent first, with bullet points
4. **Skills** — hard skills relevant to the role
5. **Education** — degree, school, graduation year

Keep it clean. One or two columns maximum. No photos, no graphics, no tables — these confuse applicant tracking systems (ATS).

## Write a Professional Summary That Hooks

Your professional summary is the first thing recruiters read. If it's vague or generic, they stop. If it's specific and compelling, they keep going.

**Bad summary:**
> "Experienced professional seeking opportunities to contribute to a dynamic organization."

**Good summary:**
> "Marketing manager with 6 years of experience driving B2B lead generation. Increased qualified pipeline by 40% at previous company through SEO and paid media. Seeking senior role at a growth-stage SaaS company."

The formula: [role] + [years of experience] + [key achievement] + [what you're looking for].

## Quantify Your Achievements

Bullet points that describe duties are forgettable. Bullet points that show results get you hired.

**Weak:**
- Managed social media accounts
- Led customer success team
- Improved website performance

**Strong:**
- Grew Instagram following from 2K to 28K in 12 months, increasing inbound leads by 34%
- Led 8-person customer success team, maintaining 97% retention rate across 200+ accounts
- Reduced page load time by 42%, boosting conversion rate by 18%

Ask yourself for every bullet: *So what? What was the result?* Numbers don't have to be perfect — estimates are fine as long as they're honest.

## Optimize for ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems)

Before a human reads your resume, software reads it first. ATS scans for keywords, formatting, and structure. If your resume fails the scan, no human ever sees it.

### How to pass ATS:

- **Use keywords from the job description.** If they say "project management," use that exact phrase — not "led projects."
- **Avoid fancy formatting.** No text boxes, columns, headers/footers, or images.
- **Use standard section headings.** "Work Experience" not "My Journey."
- **Submit as .docx or .pdf** — check the job posting for preference.

## Tailor Your Resume for Each Job

One resume for every job is why most people don't hear back. Tailoring takes 10 minutes and dramatically increases your response rate.

**What to customize:**
- Professional summary (match the role title and key requirements)
- Top skills section (mirror the job's must-haves)
- Top bullet point in each role (lead with what's most relevant)

Keep a "master resume" with everything you've ever done. For each application, pull the most relevant pieces and reframe them for that specific role.

## Keep It to One or Two Pages

Entry-level and mid-career professionals: one page. Senior professionals with 10+ years: two pages is acceptable. No one gets three pages.

If you're struggling to fit everything on one page:
- Reduce your oldest jobs to 1–2 bullet points
- Cut responsibilities entirely and keep only achievements
- Tighten your summary to 2 sentences

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should be the first thing on a resume?
Start with your contact information (name, phone, email, LinkedIn, city), followed immediately by a professional summary that hooks the recruiter in 2–3 sentences.

### How do I write bullet points that get interviews?
Focus on results, not duties—include specific numbers, percentages, or measurable outcomes (e.g., "Increased sales by 45%" instead of "Responsible for sales").

### Does my resume need a photo?
No—photos, graphics, and tables confuse applicant tracking systems and should be avoided on all resumes unless specifically requested.

### What is an ATS and why does it matter?
An ATS (Applicant Tracking System) is software that scans resumes before a human sees them; using simple formatting, no graphics, and job-relevant keywords helps your resume pass through ATS filters.

### How long should a professional summary be?
A professional summary should be 2–3 sentences maximum that clearly state your role, years of experience, one key achievement, and what position you're targeting.

### How do I choose which achievements to include?
Select accomplishments with quantifiable results that are most relevant to the job description you're applying for, prioritizing those that align with the role's requirements.
