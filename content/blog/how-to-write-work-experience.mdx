# How to Write Work Experience on a Resume (That Actually Gets You Hired)

Your work experience section is the heart of your resume. It's the first place hiring managers look, and often the only section that determines whether you move forward or get filtered out. Yet most job seekers fill this section with generic job descriptions copied from their old contracts — and wonder why they're not hearing back. Learning how to write work experience the right way means transforming a list of duties into a compelling story of impact, growth, and value.

## TL;DR

- Work experience is the most critical resume section hiring managers review first, so it must showcase impact and achievements, not just job duties.
- Include job title, company name/location, employment dates, and achievement-focused bullet points using the Context-Action-Result (CAR) formula for each role.
- Go back 10–15 years for established professionals or include all relevant roles for early-career job seekers; always contextualize employment gaps rather than hiding them.
- Transform weak bullets describing responsibilities into strong bullets with measurable results (e.g., "Grew Instagram following from 4,200 to 31,000 in 12 months by developing a content calendar").

## What to Include in Your Work Experience Section

Before you start writing, you need to know what belongs here and what doesn't. The work experience section should include every position that's professionally relevant to the role you're applying for.

For each role, you'll need four core elements:

- **Job title** — Use your official title, but if it's unusual or internal, consider adding a clarifying term in parentheses
- **Company name and location** — City and state (or country for remote roles) is standard
- **Dates of employment** — Month and year format (e.g., March 2021 – Present) is preferred
- **Bullet points describing your responsibilities and achievements**

### How Far Back Should You Go?

For most professionals, 10–15 years of experience is enough. If you're early in your career, include all relevant roles including internships, part-time work, and freelance projects. If you're a senior professional, it's acceptable — even advisable — to omit roles from the early 2000s unless they're directly relevant.

### What If You Have Gaps?

Don't hide gaps — contextualize them. If you took time off for caregiving, health, education, or travel, a brief note in your bullet points or a short summary line can frame that time productively.

## How to Write Strong Work Experience Bullet Points

This is where most resumes fall flat. Weak bullet points describe what your job was. Strong bullet points show what you accomplished in that job.

### Use the CAR Formula

The most effective structure for resume bullets is **Context – Action – Result**:

- **Context**: What was the situation or challenge?
- **Action**: What did you specifically do?
- **Result**: What happened because of your action?

Here's the difference in practice:

❌ *Responsible for managing social media accounts.*

✅ *Grew Instagram following from 4,200 to 31,000 in 12 months by developing a content calendar and launching a weekly engagement series, increasing lead generation by 40%.*

The second version tells a story. It shows initiative, strategy, and measurable outcome — exactly what hiring managers want to see.

### Lead with Strong Action Verbs

Every bullet point should begin with a powerful, specific action verb. Avoid vague openers like "responsible for," "helped with," or "worked on." Instead, use verbs that convey ownership and impact:

- **Led, Managed, Directed** — for leadership roles
- **Built, Developed, Created** — for building something new
- **Increased, Grew, Expanded** — for growth achievements
- **Reduced, Streamlined, Optimized** — for efficiency wins
- **Launched, Implemented, Executed** — for project-based work

Vary your verbs across bullets to avoid repetition and keep the reader engaged.

### Quantify Everything You Can

Numbers make your experience concrete and credible. If you can measure it, include it. Revenue, percentages, team sizes, timelines, customer counts — all of it adds weight.

If you don't have exact numbers, use estimates or ranges: *"Managed a team of approximately 10–15 contractors"* or *"Reduced processing time by roughly 30%."* Honest approximations are far more persuasive than vague language.

## How to Tailor Work Experience to Each Job

One of the biggest mistakes job seekers make is sending the same resume to every job. A customized work experience section dramatically improves your chances of passing applicant tracking systems (ATS) and resonating with human readers.

### Match Keywords from the Job Description

Read the job posting carefully and identify the key skills, tools, and phrases the employer uses. Then mirror that language in your bullet points where it's authentic and accurate. If the job calls for "cross-functional collaboration" and you've done that, use that phrase — not just "worked with other teams."

### Prioritize Relevant Experience

Reorder your bullets within each role so the most relevant achievements appear first. If you're applying for a sales leadership role, your team management bullets should come before your administrative ones — even if the administrative work took more of your time.

### Adjust Your Depth

Give more space (more bullet points, more detail) to roles that are most relevant to the job you want. A position from five years ago that directly mirrors the role you're applying for deserves more attention than your most recent job if that recent role was in a different field.

## Formatting Your Work Experience Section

Clear formatting ensures your resume is easy to scan — both by ATS software and human eyes. Keep these rules in mind:

- **Consistency is everything.** Use the same date format, the same punctuation style, and the same structure for every role.
- **4–6 bullet points per role** is the sweet spot for recent or highly relevant positions. 2–3 is fine for older or less relevant roles.
- **Keep bullets to 1–2 lines.** If a bullet runs three lines, break it up or trim it.
- **Avoid paragraphs.** Dense blocks of text get skipped. Bullets get read.
- **Use standard fonts and clean spacing.** Creative formatting can look great as a PDF but collapse completely in an ATS.

### What About Remote Work?

If a role was remote, you can note that in the location field: *Company Name, Remote* or *Company Name, New York, NY (Remote)*. This has become standard and signals flexibility and adaptability — both valued traits.

## Common Work Experience Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced professionals make these errors:

- **Listing duties instead of achievements** — The job description tells employers what you were supposed to do. Your resume should tell them what you actually did.
- **Using the same bullets for every application** — Tailoring isn't optional; it's the baseline expectation in a competitive market.
- **Leaving out volunteer work or freelance projects** — If it's relevant, it counts. Don't undersell yourself.
- **Burying the lead** — Put your strongest, most impressive bullets first in each role. Don't make hiring managers hunt for your best work.
- **Typos and inconsistencies** — A single typo in your work history can undermine an otherwise strong application. Proofread carefully, then have someone else do it too.

## Writing Work Experience When You're Changing Careers

Career changers face a unique challenge: their most recent experience may not directly relate to where they're going. The solution is to reframe, not reinvent.

Look at your past roles through the lens of your target industry. Skills like project management, client communication, data analysis, and team leadership are transferable across nearly every field. Identify those threads in your experience and highlight them prominently.

You can also supplement your work experience section with a strong summary statement at the top of your resume that explicitly connects your background to your new direction.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should I include in my work experience section?
Include your job title, company name and location, employment dates in month/year format, and 3–5 bullet points describing key responsibilities and measurable achievements for each role.

### How far back should work experience go on a resume?
Most professionals should include 10–15 years of experience; early-career job seekers should list all relevant roles including internships and freelance work, while senior professionals can omit roles from the early 2000s unless directly relevant.

### What is the CAR formula for resume bullets?
CAR stands for Context (the situation or challenge), Action (what you specifically did), and Result (the measurable outcome); this structure transforms generic duties into compelling achievement statements.

### How do I write work experience bullets that get noticed?
Replace responsibility statements with quantifiable accomplishments that show initiative and business impact, such as percentage increases, revenue generated, or time saved rather than listing what you were "responsible for."

### Does employment gap information belong in work experience?
Yes, briefly contextualize gaps with a short note explaining caregiving, health, education, or other reasons rather than leaving them unexplained, which builds trust with hiring managers.

### Should I use my unofficial job title if my actual title is confusing?
You should use your official job title, but if it's unusual or internal, add a clarifying term in parentheses (e.g., "Marketing Associate (Social Media Manager)") to improve clarity for hiring managers.

A well-crafted work experience section doesn't just list where you've been — it makes a convincing case for where you're going. Take the time to write it thoughtfully, tailor it intentionally, and update it regularly. The extra effort is what separates candidates who get interviews from those who don't.
