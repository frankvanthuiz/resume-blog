Resume length is one of the most debated questions in job searching — and one of the most misunderstood. The answer isn't complicated, but getting it wrong can cost you interviews.

Here's the definitive guide to how long your resume should be, based on where you are in your career.

## TL;DR

- Entry-level candidates should aim for one page, mid-career professionals one to two pages, and senior leaders two pages maximum.
- Recruiters scan resumes quickly, so every line must earn its place—length alone doesn't improve your chances with ATS systems.
- Two pages is only appropriate when you have 8+ years of highly relevant experience with multiple significant roles or work in technical fields requiring publications and certifications.
- Three-page resumes are rarely acceptable outside academia, federal government work, and executive roles; most corporate positions penalize poor editing over length.

## The Short Answer

- **Entry-level (0–3 years experience):** One page
- **Mid-career (3–10 years experience):** One to two pages
- **Senior/executive (10+ years experience):** Two pages, occasionally three

That's it. Most people overthink this. The real question isn't "how many pages?" — it's "does every line on this resume earn its place?"

## Why One Page Is Right for Most People

Unless you have 10+ years of directly relevant experience, one page is almost always the right choice. Here's why:

**Recruiters scan, they don't read.** More pages mean more to scan, which means more chances to lose their attention before they reach the good stuff.

**Less is more credible.** A concise, focused resume signals that you know what matters. A padded-out two-pager signals that you don't.

**ATS doesn't reward length.** Applicant tracking systems scan for keywords and relevance, not volume. A tight one-page resume with the right keywords outperforms a bloated two-pager every time.

## When Two Pages Is Appropriate

Two pages makes sense when you genuinely have more than a page of highly relevant experience. Not more content — more *relevant* content.

You should consider two pages if:
- You have 8–10+ years of experience with multiple significant roles
- You're in a technical field (engineering, research, academia) with a long list of publications, projects, or certifications
- You're applying for a senior leadership role where depth of experience matters
- Every line on the second page is pulling its weight

Two pages is **not** appropriate if:
- You're repeating yourself across roles
- You're including jobs older than 15 years in detail
- You have low-value filler (references available upon request, outdated skills, irrelevant hobbies)
- You're early or mid-career and inflating content to fill space

## The Three-Page Resume: Almost Never

Three-page resumes are appropriate for academics (CVs), federal government applications, and some executive-level positions where a full career history is expected. For most corporate roles, a three-page resume signals poor editing more than impressive experience.

If you feel you need three pages, start cutting — you almost certainly don't need them.

## How to Cut Your Resume Without Losing Impact

If you're over length, here's where to trim:

### Remove or compress old jobs
Jobs older than 10–15 years can usually be compressed to one or two bullets, or removed entirely — especially if they're not relevant to your target role.

### Cut the objective statement
Objective statements are outdated. Replace with a professional summary if you don't already have one, or remove it if your summary is already doing the job.

### Trim each bullet point
Most bullet points can be 10–15 words shorter without losing meaning. "Was responsible for the management and oversight of a team of 8 customer service professionals" becomes "Managed 8-person customer service team."

### Remove obvious skills
Everyone knows Microsoft Word. Remove skills that don't differentiate you or aren't relevant to the role.

### Cut the references line
"References available upon request" is assumed. It wastes a full line.

## What About Formatting: Can I Shrink the Font?

Shrinking your font to squeeze everything onto one page is a common mistake. Font below 10pt is hard to read and signals a resume that's trying to hide its length problem.

**Better approaches:**
- Reduce margins slightly (0.75 inches is the minimum)
- Tighten line spacing within sections
- Use a template with optimized spacing built in
- Cut content rather than squeezing it

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should an entry-level resume be?
Entry-level resumes should be one page maximum, as you likely have limited professional experience and recruiters expect conciseness from candidates early in their careers.

### Can I use two pages if I have lots of experience?
Yes, two pages is appropriate if you have 8–10+ years of directly relevant experience, but only if every line pulls its weight and isn't repetitive or filler content.

### Does resume length affect ATS screening?
No, applicant tracking systems prioritize keywords and relevance over page count, so a tight one-page resume with the right keywords outperforms a bloated two-pager.

### What should I cut to fit my resume on one page?
Remove jobs older than 15 years in detail, eliminate low-value filler like "references available upon request," and cut irrelevant skills and hobbies that don't match the job description.

### Is a three-page resume ever acceptable?
Three-page resumes are appropriate only for academic CVs, federal government applications, and some executive-level positions where full career history is expected.

### How do I know if my second page is necessary?
Your second page is necessary only if it contains highly relevant experience; if you're repeating yourself or padding content to fill space, trim it and stick to one page.
