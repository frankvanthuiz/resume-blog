Recruiters see hundreds of resumes a week. Most get 7–10 seconds of attention before being moved to the "no" pile. Understanding what recruiters actually look for — and what makes them stop and read — gives you a massive advantage over other applicants.

Here's what's really going through a recruiter's mind when they open your resume.

## The First 7 Seconds: Does This Pass the Scan?

Recruiters don't read resumes — they scan them. In the first few seconds, they're asking: *Does this person look roughly right for this role?*

What they're scanning for:

- **Job titles** — do they match the level and function of the role?
- **Company names** — recognizable brands signal credibility
- **Years of experience** — does this person have the right seniority?
- **Layout clarity** — can I find information quickly?

If any of these are unclear or missing, the resume gets skipped. That's it. This is why formatting and structure matter before content does.

## Relevance to the Role

The number one thing recruiters look for is simple: *Is this person relevant for this specific job?*

Generic resumes fail here. A resume written for "any marketing role" reads like it was written for no marketing role.

Recruiters want to see:
- A professional summary that matches the job title and key requirements
- Skills that reflect the job description's must-haves
- Experience in the same industry or function
- Keywords that mirror the posting (because ATS filtered for them first)

**The fix:** Spend 10 minutes tailoring your summary and top skills for each application. It's the single highest-ROI action you can take.

## Measurable Achievements, Not Just Duties

This is where most resumes lose recruiters. A list of responsibilities tells them what your job was. Achievements tell them how good you are at it.

**What recruiters don't want to see:**
- "Responsible for managing social media"
- "Handled customer complaints"
- "Assisted with project coordination"

**What makes them stop and take notice:**
- "Grew LinkedIn page from 500 to 12,000 followers in 8 months"
- "Resolved 95% of customer complaints within 24 hours, achieving 4.9/5 satisfaction score"
- "Coordinated 6-month product launch across 4 teams, delivered 2 weeks ahead of schedule"

Numbers don't need to be exact. Reasonable estimates signal accountability and self-awareness.

## ATS Compliance: Getting Past the Algorithm

Many recruiters never see your resume if it doesn't pass the applicant tracking system first. ATS software scans for keywords, proper formatting, and parseable structure.

What causes ATS rejection:
- Missing keywords from the job description
- Tables, text boxes, or graphics that confuse parsing
- Unusual section headers ("My Experience" instead of "Work Experience")
- Submitting the wrong file format

What recruiters see when ATS passes your resume: a clean, correctly parsed document. What they see when it doesn't: nothing.

## Clean, Professional Formatting

Recruiters don't have time to decode a cluttered resume. They want information to be findable in seconds.

Signs of poor formatting that hurt you:
- Walls of text with no white space
- Inconsistent fonts, sizes, or bullet styles
- Missing or unclear section dividers
- Contact info buried at the bottom

Signs of formatting that gets you hired:
- Plenty of white space
- Clear hierarchy (name → summary → experience → skills)
- Consistent formatting throughout
- Contact info front and center at the top

## No Errors

Typos and grammar mistakes signal carelessness. Recruiters filling professional roles have zero tolerance for them. One typo doesn't necessarily end your chances — but two or three will.

Before sending any resume:
- Run it through Grammarly
- Read it aloud (catches errors your eyes skip)
- Have someone else read it
- Check your own name, phone number, and email are correct

## A LinkedIn Profile That Matches

Many recruiters check LinkedIn immediately after opening your resume. If your job titles, dates, or companies don't match — that's a red flag.

Keep your LinkedIn profile consistent with your resume. You don't need to copy it word for word, but the facts should align.

## What Recruiters Rarely Care About

Knowing what recruiters *don't* prioritize saves you time:

- **Objective statements** — outdated, replace with a professional summary
- **References available upon request** — assumed, wastes space
- **Every job you've ever had** — trim anything older than 10–15 years
- **Fancy graphics and design** — unless you're in a creative field, keep it clean
- **Hobbies and interests** — only if directly relevant to the role
