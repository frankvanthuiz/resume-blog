# ATS Resume Tips: 10 Rules That Get You Through

You spent hours perfecting your resume — strong verbs, clean formatting, solid experience — and still heard nothing. No call, no email, no acknowledgment. Chances are, your resume never reached human eyes at all. Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) scan and filter resumes before a recruiter ever sees them, and most job seekers have no idea how to play by the rules. These 10 ATS resume tips will change that. Follow them, and you'll stop getting filtered out and start getting interviews.

## TL;DR

- Use single-column layouts with standard fonts and no graphics to ensure ATS systems can read your resume correctly.
- Mirror exact keywords and phrases from the job description to improve your ATS ranking and keyword match score.
- Avoid tables, text boxes, and complex formatting that ATS parsers cannot properly interpret or may skip entirely.
- Follow all 10 ATS rules consistently to prevent your resume from being filtered out before reaching a recruiter's eyes.

## What Is an ATS and Why It Controls Your Fate

An Applicant Tracking System is software that employers use to collect, sort, and rank resumes automatically. When you apply online, your resume is parsed by this system — meaning it's broken down and read like a database entry, not a designed document. The ATS assigns scores based on keyword matches, formatting clarity, and data fields like job titles and dates.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: studies suggest that **75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a recruiter sees them**. That's not because the candidates were underqualified — it's because their resumes weren't formatted in a way the software could read.

Understanding this system is step one. Now let's talk about how to beat it.

## Rule 1: Use a Simple, Clean Layout

Fancy resume templates with columns, text boxes, graphics, and headers look impressive to the human eye — but they're a nightmare for ATS parsers. Most systems read left to right, top to bottom, in a single column. When they hit a two-column layout or an embedded text box, they either scramble the information or skip it entirely.

### What to do instead:
- Use a **single-column format** with clear sections
- Stick to standard fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman
- Avoid tables, graphics, logos, and icons
- Use simple horizontal lines for section dividers if needed

Clean and readable isn't boring — it's strategic.

## Rule 2: Match Keywords from the Job Description

ATS systems rank resumes by how well they match the job posting. If the job description says "project management" and your resume says "managing projects," you might not score as high as someone who used the exact phrase.

### How to do keyword matching the right way:
- Read the job description carefully and highlight repeated terms
- Mirror the exact language used — don't paraphrase
- Include both spelled-out terms and abbreviations (e.g., "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)")
- Place keywords in your skills section, work experience, and professional summary

This isn't about stuffing keywords robotically — it's about speaking the employer's language.

## Rule 3: Use Standard Section Headers

ATS systems are trained to look for specific section titles. If you rename "Work Experience" to "Where I've Made an Impact," the system may not recognize the section at all — and your entire job history could get skipped.

Stick to these proven, ATS-safe headers:
- **Work Experience** (or Experience)
- **Education**
- **Skills**
- **Certifications**
- **Summary** or **Professional Summary**

Creativity is great in a cover letter. Your resume headers should be conventional.

## Rule 4: Submit in the Right File Format

When given the choice, submit your resume as a **.docx file** (Microsoft Word format). Most ATS platforms parse Word documents more reliably than PDFs. While PDF support has improved, it's still inconsistent across different systems.

If the job posting specifies a format, follow it exactly. When in doubt, .docx is the safer bet.

## Rule 5: Write a Keyword-Rich Professional Summary

Your professional summary sits at the top of your resume and is one of the first sections the ATS scans. This is prime real estate. Use it to include your most important keywords naturally, while also telling your story concisely.

Aim for 3–4 sentences that cover your job title, years of experience, core skills, and a notable achievement. Avoid vague phrases like "results-driven professional" — they're filler that no ATS is scoring you on.

## Rule 6: Quantify Your Achievements

While ATS systems primarily filter on keywords and structure, human recruiters eventually review the resumes that pass the scan. Quantified achievements make you stand out at that stage — and some ATS systems are beginning to score for specificity.

Instead of "Managed a team," write "Managed a team of 8 educators, improving department test scores by 22% over two years." Numbers add credibility and context that generic statements never can.

## Rule 7: Avoid Headers and Footers for Contact Info

Many job seekers put their name and contact information in the document header — it looks clean and saves space. But ATS software often can't read document headers and footers. Your name, email, and phone number may not get captured at all.

Place all contact information in the **main body** of the document, at the very top of the page.

## Rule 8: Don't Use Images or Headshots

Even if it's a professional headshot, images cannot be read by ATS systems. They take up space, add file size, and often cause parsing errors. In many countries and industries, including a photo can also introduce unconscious bias — so there's an ethical reason to leave it off too.

Keep your resume text-only.

## Rule 9: Tailor Your Resume for Each Application

One resume does not fit all jobs — especially when ATS is involved. Each job posting has unique language, requirements, and priority skills. A resume tailored to one posting may score poorly on another.

Yes, this takes more time. But the strategy works:
- Keep a **master resume** with all your experience and skills
- For each application, duplicate it and adjust the summary, skills, and keyword language to match the specific job description
- Focus on the top 5–8 keywords from each posting

Even 20 minutes of tailoring per application can significantly improve your ATS score.

## Rule 10: Use Bullet Points, Not Paragraphs

ATS systems parse bullet points more reliably than dense paragraphs. Bullet points also make it easier for human reviewers to quickly scan your experience. Use them consistently in your work experience section.

Start each bullet with a strong action verb: *designed, implemented, led, reduced, increased, developed, managed, coordinated*. Keep bullets concise — one to two lines each — and make sure every bullet communicates either a responsibility or a result.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is an ATS and how does it affect my job application?
An Applicant Tracking System is software that automatically scans, parses, and ranks resumes before a recruiter sees them, filtering candidates based on keyword matches and formatting clarity.

### Why do 75% of resumes get rejected by ATS?
Most resumes are rejected not because candidates are underqualified, but because their formatting—like two-column layouts, graphics, or complex templates—prevents the ATS parser from reading the content correctly.

### How do I use keyword matching to improve my ATS score?
Read the job description carefully, identify repeated terms, and mirror the exact language in your skills section and work experience rather than paraphrasing similar concepts.

### Should I use fancy resume templates with graphics and columns?
No; fancy templates with multiple columns, text boxes, graphics, and logos confuse ATS parsers and often cause information to be scrambled or skipped entirely.

### What fonts and formatting should I use on an ATS-friendly resume?
Stick to standard fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman, use a single-column format, and avoid tables, graphics, and icons—simple horizontal lines are acceptable for section dividers.

### Should I include both acronyms and spelled-out terms in my resume?
Yes; include both versions (for example, "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)") to ensure the ATS catches keyword matches regardless of how the employer searches for that skill.

Getting through ATS isn't about gaming the system — it's about presenting your qualifications in a way that both software and humans can understand and appreciate. These 10 ATS resume tips give you a clear, actionable framework: simplify your formatting, speak the language of the job description, and put your best information where the system can find it. Apply these rules consistently, and your resume will spend a lot less time in the rejection pile and a lot more time in front of the people who can actually hire you.
