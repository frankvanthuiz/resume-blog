# How to Check if Your Resume is ATS Compatible

You've spent hours crafting the perfect resume — polished language, strong bullet points, the right experience. But if it can't get past an Applicant Tracking System (ATS), none of that effort matters. Studies suggest that over 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS software before a human ever reads them. Knowing how to check if your resume is ATS compatible isn't optional anymore — it's a critical step in your job search strategy.

## What Is an ATS and Why Does It Matter?

An Applicant Tracking System is software that employers use to collect, filter, and rank resumes before passing them to hiring managers. When you submit your resume online, there's a good chance it goes straight into an ATS database — not a human inbox.

The ATS scans your resume for relevant keywords, checks the formatting for readability, and scores your application based on how well it matches the job description. If your resume trips up the parser — through unusual formatting, missing keywords, or incompatible file types — it gets filtered out automatically.

### How ATS Parsing Works

The parser reads your resume like a machine, not a person. It's looking for:

- **Section headers** it recognizes (like "Work Experience" or "Education")
- **Keywords** that match the job description
- **Clean text** it can extract without confusion
- **Standard formatting** that doesn't interfere with data extraction

When any of these elements are missing or broken, the ATS either misreads your information or ranks you lower than candidates with simpler, cleaner resumes.

## How to Check if Your Resume is ATS Compatible

The good news: you don't need to guess. There are concrete, actionable ways to test your resume's ATS compatibility right now.

### 1. Copy and Paste Your Resume Into a Plain Text Editor

This is the simplest ATS compatibility test you can do for free. Open Notepad (Windows) or TextEdit in plain text mode (Mac), then paste your entire resume.

What you're looking for:

- **Does the text appear in the right order?** Name, contact info, summary, experience, education — if the sequence is scrambled, an ATS will be confused too.
- **Are special characters showing up as symbols or question marks?** Fancy bullets, em-dashes, and icons often don't translate cleanly.
- **Are columns merging into one block?** Multi-column layouts collapse in plain text, which means an ATS may jumble your job titles with your dates or company names.

If your resume looks garbled in plain text, it's almost certainly causing problems with ATS scanners.

### 2. Run It Through a Free ATS Scanner

Several tools will analyze your resume against a job description and give you a compatibility score. Some popular options include Jobscan, Resume Worded, and TopResume's free review tool.

Here's how to use them effectively:

- Paste in the actual job description from the role you're applying for
- Upload your resume in its original format
- Review the keyword match percentage and missing skills
- Pay attention to formatting warnings, not just keyword gaps

Aim for a keyword match score of at least 60–70% before submitting to any position. Below that, your resume may never reach a recruiter.

### 3. Check Your File Format

File format matters more than most people realize. The safest formats for ATS compatibility are:

- **.docx (Microsoft Word)** — the most universally accepted format
- **.pdf** — acceptable with many modern ATS platforms, but only if it's a text-based PDF, not a scanned image

Avoid submitting resumes as image files (.jpg, .png), Google Docs links, or heavily designed PDFs created in tools like Canva or Adobe InDesign. These often fail to parse correctly because the text is embedded in graphics or layers that ATS software can't read.

## Common ATS Compatibility Mistakes to Fix Right Now

Even well-intentioned resumes make these errors. Run through this checklist and correct anything that applies.

### Avoid Headers and Footers for Key Information

Many ATS systems can't read text placed in the header or footer sections of a Word document. If your contact information — phone number, email, LinkedIn URL — lives in the document header, it may be completely invisible to the ATS.

**Fix it:** Move all essential contact details into the body of the document.

### Use Standard Section Titles

Creative section headers like "My Journey," "Where I've Been," or "Skills Arsenal" sound memorable to humans but are meaningless to an ATS. The software is trained to recognize conventional labels.

**Use these instead:**
- Work Experience (not "Professional Journey")
- Education (not "My Academic Background")
- Skills (not "What I Bring to the Table")
- Certifications or Licenses

### Don't Rely on Tables or Text Boxes

Tables and text boxes are invisible to many ATS parsers. If you've formatted your skills section or contact info inside a table, that content may be completely skipped during parsing.

**Fix it:** Replace tables and text boxes with simple, left-aligned plain text.

### Match Keywords From the Job Description

ATS software is keyword-driven. If the job posting says "project management" and your resume says "overseeing projects," the ATS may not make that connection. Use the exact language from the job description wherever it's truthful and applicable.

Scan the job posting for:
- Required technical skills
- Software or tools mentioned
- Certifications or credentials listed
- Industry-specific terminology

Incorporate those terms naturally into your work experience bullets and skills section.

## How to Optimize Your Resume Keywords Without Keyword Stuffing

There's a right way and a wrong way to include keywords. Stuffing a resume with repeated terms — especially in white text or at tiny font sizes — used to fool older ATS systems. Modern platforms flag this as suspicious, and recruiters who eventually read your resume will notice immediately.

Instead, optimize strategically:

- **Customize for each job application.** A generic resume rarely scores well. Spend 10–15 minutes tailoring your resume to each position.
- **Use keywords in context.** "Managed a team of five using agile project management methodology" is far more effective than simply listing "agile, project management" in a keyword dump.
- **Include both spelled-out terms and acronyms.** Write "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" once so both versions are recognized.
- **Mirror the job title.** If the posting says "Marketing Coordinator" and you have that experience, use that exact phrase somewhere in your resume.
