# Student Resume Example: How to Write a Resume With Little to No Experience

Landing your first job, internship, or part-time role can feel overwhelming when you stare at a blank page and wonder what on earth to put on your resume. The truth is, every professional started exactly where you are right now. A strong student resume doesn't require years of work history — it requires knowing how to frame what you *do* have in a way that speaks to employers. This guide walks you through a practical student resume example, explains what to include in each section, and gives you the tools to create a resume that actually gets noticed.

## TL;DR

- Student resumes should be one page and focus on education, transferable skills, and relevant experience like projects, volunteering, or part-time work.
- Format your resume with standard fonts (10–12pt), clear section headers, and adequate margins; save as PDF and avoid tables or graphics that ATS software cannot read.
- Lead with contact information including a professional email, phone number, location, and LinkedIn URL if available.
- Every line must earn its place—recruiters evaluate student resumes for potential, reliability, and learning ability rather than extensive work history.

## What Makes a Student Resume Different

A student resume follows the same basic principles as any professional resume, but the emphasis shifts. Instead of leading with a long work history, you lead with your education, highlight transferable skills, and showcase any experience you've gained through school projects, volunteering, extracurriculars, or part-time jobs.

Recruiters reviewing student resumes understand you haven't had decades to build a career. What they're looking for is potential, reliability, and the ability to learn. Your job is to give them evidence of all three — even if that evidence comes from a class project or a summer babysitting gig.

### The Right Length for a Student Resume

Keep it to one page. Full stop. You don't have the experience to justify two pages, and a tightly edited single-page resume signals confidence and clarity. Every line should earn its place.

## How to Format a Student Resume

Before you write a single word, get the format right. A clean, readable layout makes a strong first impression and helps applicant tracking systems (ATS) parse your information correctly.

**Recommended formatting basics:**
- Use a standard font like Calibri, Arial, or Georgia in 10–12pt
- Keep margins between 0.5 and 1 inch
- Use bold headers to separate sections clearly
- Save and submit as a PDF unless the job posting specifies otherwise
- Avoid columns, tables, graphics, or text boxes — ATS software often can't read them

A simple, well-organized layout will always outperform a flashy template with zero substance.

## Student Resume Example: Section-by-Section Breakdown

Here's how to build each section of your student resume from the ground up.

### Contact Information

Put your name at the top in a slightly larger font. Below it, include:
- A professional email address (firstname.lastname@email.com, not "partyanimal99")
- Your phone number
- City and state (no need for your full address)
- LinkedIn profile URL (if you have one)
- A link to a portfolio or GitHub if relevant to the job

### Resume Objective or Summary

For students with limited experience, a brief objective statement (2–3 sentences) helps contextualize your resume and tells the employer exactly what you're looking for and what you bring to the table.

**Example:**
*Motivated marketing student at the University of Michigan seeking a summer internship where I can apply my skills in social media strategy, content creation, and data analysis. Dean's List recipient with hands-on experience managing a 2,000-follower Instagram account for a campus organization.*

Keep it specific. Generic statements like "hardworking team player seeking an opportunity to grow" waste valuable space and signal a lack of effort.

### Education

For most students, education is your biggest selling point — so put it near the top.

Include:
- School name and location
- Degree and major (and minor, if relevant)
- Expected graduation date
- GPA (if it's 3.0 or higher — leave it off if it's not)
- Relevant coursework (optional, but useful if you lack work experience)
- Honors, awards, or Dean's List recognition

**Example:**
*Bachelor of Science in Business Administration | Minor in Marketing*
*University of Texas at Austin | Expected May 2026*
*GPA: 3.6 | Dean's List (3 semesters)*
*Relevant Coursework: Digital Marketing, Consumer Behavior, Financial Accounting*

### Work Experience (Including Non-Traditional Experience)

This is where students often freeze up. Here's the key insight: almost any job counts. Retail, food service, babysitting, tutoring, camp counseling — these roles all demonstrate real skills like customer service, time management, communication, and problem-solving.

For each position, include:
- Job title
- Employer name and location
- Dates of employment
- 2–4 bullet points describing your responsibilities and achievements

**Use strong action verbs and quantify where possible.**

**Example:**
*Barista | Starbucks, Austin, TX | June 2023 – Present*
- Prepared 100+ beverages per shift while maintaining accuracy and speed during peak hours
- Trained 3 new team members on drink preparation and customer service standards
- Consistently received positive customer feedback, contributing to a 4.8-star store rating

If you have zero paid work experience, use this section for volunteer roles, freelance projects, or significant school positions.

### Skills

Create a concise skills section that lists both hard and soft skills relevant to the role you're applying for. Don't just dump every skill you've ever heard of — tailor this list to the job description.

**Example skills for a student resume:**
- Microsoft Office Suite (Word, Excel, PowerPoint)
- Google Analytics (beginner)
- Social media management (Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn)
- Spanish (conversational)
- Time management, written communication, team collaboration

### Extracurricular Activities and Campus Involvement

This section is your secret weapon as a student. Leadership roles in clubs, athletic teams, student government, or honors societies demonstrate initiative, commitment, and the ability to juggle multiple responsibilities.

**Example:**
*Vice President | Student Marketing Association | Sept 2023 – Present*
- Organized 6 networking events per semester, averaging 80+ student attendees
- Led a team of 5 committee members to redesign the association's website, increasing traffic by 40%

### Projects and Academic Work

If you've completed a class project, case competition, research paper, or independent study that's directly relevant to the job, include it. This is especially valuable for students in technical fields like computer science, data analytics, or engineering.

**Example:**
*Market Analysis Project | Digital Marketing Course | Spring 2024*
- Conducted a competitive analysis of 5 brands in the sustainable fashion industry
- Developed a social media strategy that earned first place in the class competition

## Common Mistakes to Avoid on a Student Resume

Even a well-intentioned resume can hurt your chances if you make these errors:

- **Using a generic objective statement** that could apply to any job at any company
- **Listing responsibilities without results** — always try to quantify your impact
- **Including a photo** — unless you're applying internationally where it's expected, leave it off
- **Using an unprofessional email address** — this is surprisingly common and immediately off-putting
- **Forgetting to tailor your resume** for each application — swap in keywords from the job description

## Tailoring Your Student Resume for Different Opportunities

One resume does not fit all. If you're applying for an internship at a marketing agency, emphasize your social media skills and class projects. If you're applying for a lab assistant position, highlight relevant coursework, research experience, and technical tools you've used. Spend 10–15 minutes customizing your resume for each application — it makes a measurable difference in your response rate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should a student include on a resume with no work experience?
Focus on education, class projects, volunteer work, extracurricular activities, relevant skills, and any leadership roles or achievements that demonstrate potential and reliability.

### How long should a student resume be?
A student resume should be exactly one page; you don't have enough experience to justify two pages, and a single-page format signals clarity and confidence.

### What is the best font and formatting for a student resume?
Use standard fonts like Calibri, Arial, or Georgia in 10–12pt size, maintain 0.5–1 inch margins, use bold headers for sections, and save as a PDF to ensure ATS compatibility.

### Should a student resume include a full home address?
No, only include your city and state on a student resume; a full home address is unnecessary and outdated for modern job applications.

### How do I make a student resume stand out to employers?
Frame your experiences—even small ones—as evidence of potential, reliability, and learning ability by using action verbs and highlighting transferable skills relevant to the position.

### Can I use tables, columns, or graphics on a student resume?
Avoid tables, columns, graphics, and text boxes because applicant tracking systems (ATS) often cannot read them; keep your layout simple and clean instead.

Writing a student resume is less about what you've done and more about how you present it. Every experience, whether it's managing a club's social media or working weekend shifts at a grocery store, has taught you something valuable. Your job is to make that value visible. Start with the structure outlined in this student resume example, tailor it to each opportunity, and keep refining it as you gain new experiences. You're not starting from zero — you're starting from exactly where you need to be.
