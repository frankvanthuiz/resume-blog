Most resumes don't fail because the applicant isn't qualified. They fail because of avoidable mistakes that signal inexperience, carelessness, or a lack of effort. Recruiters see these errors every day — and they act as instant disqualifiers.

Here are the 10 resume mistakes that get you rejected, and exactly how to fix each one.

## 1. Using a Generic, Untailored Resume

Sending the same resume to every job is the most common — and most damaging — mistake job seekers make. Recruiters can tell immediately when a resume wasn't written for their role.

**The fix:** Customize your professional summary and top skills for each application. Mirror language from the job description. It takes 10 minutes and can double your response rate.

## 2. Listing Duties Instead of Achievements

"Responsible for managing the marketing budget" tells a recruiter what your job was. It doesn't tell them whether you were any good at it.

**The fix:** Rewrite every bullet point as an outcome. "Managed $200K marketing budget, reducing cost-per-lead by 28% while increasing qualified pipeline by $1.2M." Numbers, results, impact.

## 3. Failing the ATS Test

Applicant tracking systems screen most resumes before a human sees them. If your resume uses tables, text boxes, unusual fonts, or missing keywords, it gets filtered out — and you never know why you didn't hear back.

## 4. A Weak or Missing Professional Summary

Many candidates skip the summary entirely, or write something so vague it's useless: *"Motivated professional seeking challenging opportunities."* This wastes the most-read section of your resume.

**The fix:** Write a 2–3 sentence summary that states your role, years of experience, top achievement, and what you're looking for. Make it specific. Make it about the value you bring — not what you want.

## 5. Typos and Grammar Errors

One typo might be forgiven. Two or three will end your application. Recruiters interpret errors as a signal of how you'll perform on the job — especially for roles requiring written communication.

**The fix:** Use Grammarly. Read your resume aloud. Have someone else proofread it. Check your contact information carefully — the wrong phone number or email address on an otherwise great resume is a career-limiting move.

## 6. Wrong Length

A three-page resume from someone with five years of experience tells a recruiter you don't know what's important. A one-page resume from someone with 20 years of experience tells them you haven't been doing much.

**The fix:**
- 0–10 years experience: one page
- 10+ years experience: two pages maximum
- Executive/senior leadership: two pages, occasionally three

Cut old jobs to 1–2 bullets. Remove everything that isn't relevant to the target role.

## 7. Poor Formatting and Visual Clutter

A resume with four different fonts, random bold text, no white space, and misaligned sections is exhausting to read. Recruiters won't work to decode it.

**The fix:** Use one font (or two at most), consistent formatting throughout, clear section dividers, and enough white space to make the content breathable. Or use a professionally designed resume template — formatting is already handled.

## 8. Irrelevant Information

Listing hobbies, high school education (for college graduates), jobs from 20 years ago, or an "objective statement" wastes valuable space and ages your resume.

**The fix:** Every line should serve one purpose: making the case that you're the right person for this specific role. If it doesn't do that, cut it.

## 9. No LinkedIn Profile — or a Mismatched One

Most recruiters check LinkedIn immediately after opening your resume. If you don't have a profile, that's a red flag. If your profile contradicts your resume — different dates, different job titles — that's worse.

**The fix:** Keep LinkedIn active and consistent with your resume. Update it every time you update your resume. Add a link to it in your contact section.

## 10. Applying Without Proofreading the Whole Package

A great resume paired with a cover letter addressed to the wrong company, or a job application with a typo in your email address, will get you rejected. The details matter end-to-end.

**The fix:** Before submitting any application, read every piece of it from top to bottom. Check that names, companies, and roles mentioned are correct for this specific application.
